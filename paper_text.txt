ABSTRACT
Semantic communications, a promising approach for agent-human and agent-agent inter-
actions, typically operate at a feature level, lacking true semantic understanding. This paper explores
understanding-level semantic communications (ULSC), transforming visual data into human-intelligible
semantic content. We employ an image caption neural network (ICNN) to derive semantic representations
from visual data, expressed as natural language descriptions. These are further refined using a pre-trained
large language model (LLM) for importance quantification and semantic error correction. The subsequent
semantic importance-aware communications (SIAC) aim to minimize semantic loss while respecting trans-
mission delay constraints, exemplified through adaptive modulation and coding strategies. At the receiving
end, LLM-based semantic error correction is utilized. If visual data recreation is desired, a pre-trained gener-
ative artificial intelligence (AI) model can regenerate it using the corrected descriptions. We assess semantic
similarities between transmitted and recovered content, demonstrating ULSC’s superior ability to convey
semantic understanding compared to feature-level semantic communications (FLSC). ULSC’s conversion
of visual data to natural language facilitates various cognitive tasks, leveraging human knowledge bases.
Additionally, this method enhances privacy, as neither original data nor features are directly transmitted.

3) NATURAL LANGUAGE REPRESENTATIONS
Compared to feature-level systems, this approach bridges the
semantic gap between low-level data and high-level human-
related meanings. It uses a natural language representation
to map data into semantic domains that can be understood
by humans. This approach has been explored in a num-
ber of works. For example, [26] studies how to use deep
learning techniques to transform images into informative text
descriptions. Reference [27] further explores how this con-
version process can be optimized to produce a more accurate
text representation. Focusing on the wireless communication
environment, [28] studies how to effectively transmit these
semantically rich text descriptions. This approach not only
improves the quality of interaction between machines and
humans, but also enhances the semantic communication abil-
ity between machines.
B. CONTRIBUTIONS OF THIS WORK
This paper introduces a novel understanding-level semantic
communications (ULSC) system, adeptly designed for seam-
less integration with existing communication networks. The
system is characterized by its utilization of advanced com-
ponents such as pre-trained image caption neural networks
(ICNNs), and large language models (LLMs). Compared
with existing mixed image-text transmission systems [26],
[27], [28], our proposed ULSC not only focuses on feature
extraction and encoding, but also prioritizes processing and
transmission of the most critical semantic information by
introducing a semantic importance aware mechanism. The
contributions of this work are outlined as follows:
• This work introduces a novel method for quantifying
the semantic importance of transmitted messages using
pre-trained LLMs. Unlike existing methods that focus

• We present a semantic error correction mechanism

our approach significantly reduces semantic loss.
• Our framework enhances privacy protection by trans-
forming raw visual data into high-level semantic repre-
sentations, which are more difficult to reverse-engineer.
This provides a higher level of security compared
to traditional feature-level communication systems,
as unauthorized entities cannot easily infer sensitive
information from intercepted transmissions. We demon-

II. SYSTEM MODEL AND PROBLEM DESCRIPTION
We consider an understanding-level semantic communication
system, as shown in Fig. 1. The system aims to convey
the semantic essence of visual information in the context
of limited communication bandwidth and network resources.
This is achieved by transforming visual data into natural lan-
guage descriptions and employing semantic error correction
techniques.
A. SYSTEM MODEL
The ULSC system model includes a transmitter with a visual
input function that processes visual data through an image
captioning neural network (ICNN) and generates natural lan-
guage descriptions. Let S ∈RN represent the input visual
data, where N is the dimension of the image data. The ICNN
processes this data and converts it into a natural language
description X ∈ZD, where Z is the vocabulary and D is
the number of words. The mathematical formula for this
conversion is:
X = fα(S),
(1)
where fα(·) is the ICNN function, parameterized by the neural
network weight α.
For transmission, the natural language description X is
divided into L frames, each of which is encoded as a
binary sequence by source coding and channel coding. Let
{b1, b2, · · · , bL} represent the encoded binary sequence of L
frames. These frames are then transmitted over the network.
It is worth mentioning that in order to optimize transmission,
we prioritize semantically important information.
During transmission, the encoded frames {b1, b2, · · · , bL}
may have errors or be lost. The receiver uses a semantic
error correction mechanism based on a large language model
(LLM) to recover the lost frames, ensuring that the trans-
mitted semantic information is preserved. The mathematical
expression of semantic error correction is:

After obtaining the corrected natural language description
˜X, it will be passed to the final receiver. The receiver may
be a visually impaired individual who receives information
through acoustic channels. Alternatively, the receiver could
be a generative AI model with natural language representa-

formats of content. fβ(·) denotes speech generation model,
generative AI model, or some other model, and β represents
its parameters.
B. PROBLEM DESCRIPTION
In ULSC system, raw visual data is first transformed into a
series of natural language descriptions. These descriptions
are then divided into multiple data frames for encoding and
preparation for transmission. However, in the process of net-
work transmission, these encoded frames may be wrong or
lost due to various factors such as noise interference and
signal attenuation. To address this challenge, we employ
semantic error correction techniques on the receiving end
to recover lost or damaged frames as much as possible,
thereby maximizing the retention of transmitted semantic
information.

III. TEXTUAL ENCODER AND FRAME IMPORTANCE
QUANTIFICATION
This section delves into the intricacies of the textual encoder
within the visual data-to-text conversion process and the
methodology for quantifying frame importance, which are
fundamental components of the ULSC transmitters.
A. VISUAL DATA TO NATURAL LANGUAGE CONVERSION
The primary objective of ULSC is to facilitate the compre-
hension of visual data for visually impaired individuals or
generative AI systems by utilizing expansive human knowl-
edge bases. The transformation of visual data into natural
language is a critical and intricate process within ULSC,
encompassing aspects of computer vision (CV) and natural
language processing (NLP). This complex process, referred
to as image captioning in the field of deep learning, requires
a deep understanding of visual content to generate accurate
and meaningful descriptions.
Significant advancements have been made in addressing
this challenge. Notable approaches include the encoder-
decoder model, as demonstrated in works by Vinyals et al.
[29] and Karpathy et al. [30]. These models employ convo-
lutional neural networks (CNNs) to extract image features
and recurrent neural networks (RNNs) or long short-term
memory (LSTM) networks to generate corresponding textual
descriptions. To enhance the accuracy and relevance of these
descriptions, an attention mechanism has been integrated into
the encoder-decoder framework. This mechanism allows the
model to focus on different segments of the image during the
generation process, as detailed in the studies by Xu et al. [31]
and Li et al. [32].
Building on these advancements, our research has adopted
the pre-trained ICNN proposed by Li et al. [32]. Fig. 2(a)
shows the workflow of the ICNN. The input image is
divided into a number of small blocks, which are con-
verted into a high-dimensional feature representation after
passing through the image encoder. These image feature vec-
tors are then passed to an image-based text decoder, which
generates a text description related to the image content.
Fig. 2(b) shows the structure of the image encoder and
image-grounded text decoder in detail. The image encoder
consists of N Transformer layers. Each Transformer layer
includes a Self-Attention layer, Feed Forward network, and
LayerNorm. These layers extract image features by Self-
Attention mechanism, and further process them by Feed
FIGURE 2. The adopted pre-trained ICNN and its details.
Forward network, and finally generate image feature vectors.
In image-grounded text decoder, each Transformer layer also
contains LayerNorm, Feed Forward network, and Causal
Self-Attention layer. In addition, a Cross Attention layer is
added, which is used to combine image features and text
information to generate text descriptions related to the image
content.
Notably, the generated text’s length is adjusted according
to the complexity of the image. For simpler images, the text
generated will be shorter, while for more complex images
with richer semantic content, the text length increases accord-
ingly. This dynamic text generation mechanism ensures that
the description is neither too long nor too short. The image
encoder also analyzes the image’s complexity by evaluating
various aspects such as the number of objects, scene diversity,
and the relationships among visual elements. The encoder
generates feature vectors that inform the text decoder about
these aspects, enabling the system to prioritize semantically
important elements in the image. For example, when pro-
cessing an image with multiple objects or scenes, the model
identifies and emphasizes key objects and their interactions,
and generates text that reflects these important elements.
Further details of the model and its implementation can be
found in [32].
In traditional communication systems, there is a risk
of visual privacy disclosure when transmitting raw images
or low-level features. An attacker can use methods such
as model inversion [7] to reconstruct the original image
(e.g., facial image), and then obtain the user’s skin color

B. FRAME IMPORTANCE QUANTIFICATION
The concept of frame importance is pivotal in semantic
importance-aware communications. It pertains to the rel-
evance and significance of data within a frame for the
comprehensive understanding of the overall context. Due to
its content-dependent and dynamic nature, the quantification
of semantic importance is intricate and often requires deep
contextual understanding and domain-specific knowledge.
Establishing universal metrics for this quantification is chal-
lenging, as it may vary across different contexts.
In our approach, we amalgamate human judgment with
automated methods, including natural language processing
techniques, machine learning algorithms, and user feedback,
to quantify the semantic importance of data within frames.
This multifaceted approach is a subject of ongoing research.
Specifically, we utilize pre-trained language models, such as
ChatGPT [33] and BERT [34], to measure the importance of
words and frames based on the semantic variations resulting
from their erasure, as detailed in [18].
Our method involves comparing the original message,
denoted as m0, with a modified version, ml, where the lth
frame is absent but replaced with a completion frame gener-
ated by a LLM. The semantic importance of the lth frame is
then quantified as:

using the cosine similarity of the embeddings generated
by a pre-trained BERT model, Bψ(·), for both messages.
This approach ensures that the quantification of importance
reflects the ability of semantic error correction mechanisms
to recover or predict missing information.
Notably, this method allows the transmitter to indepen-
dently assess the importance of words or frames without
requiring interaction with the receiver. By simulating a
semantic error corrector, the transmitter can determine the
potential for each word or frame to be accurately recon-
structed or predicted by the receiver’s error correction
system.
Table 1 presents a comparative analysis between our pro-
posed method, which includes semantic error correction, and

this comparison is the prevalence of zero importance scores
for most words in the context of semantic error correction.
This outcome indicates that many words, when lost, can be
effectively predicted and completed by the receiver’s error
correction mechanisms. Consequently, the ULSC framework
can prioritize transmitting words or frames that are less likely
to be accurately reconstructed if lost, thereby enhancing com-
munication efficiency and reliability.
This approach represents a significant advancement
in the field of semantic communications, particularly
in applications involving ULSC, as it provides a more
nuanced understanding of data importance and optimizes
data transmission based on the receiver’s error correction
capabilities.

overall efficiency of the system. In this framework, frames
of greater semantic importance are transmitted or processed
in precedence to those of lesser significance.

V. SEMANTIC CORRECTION AND VISUAL DATA
GENERATION
The process of receiving semantically important signal rep-
resentations, which are detected as bits, involves error
correction for rectifiable errors, while uncorrectable frames
are discarded. This study proposes a novel approach that
leverages a LLM for word completion in scenarios where
frames are lost.
A. SEMANTIC CORRECTION METHODOLOGY
Despite efforts to prioritize the transmission of semanti-
cally significant frames and quantify their importance, the
reliability of information transmission cannot be guaran-
teed due to unpredictable factors such as wireless channel
fading and noise interference. These factors can distort or
result in the loss of information during transmission. In the
ULSC process, the intermediate state is the natural language
description. If this is not transmitted reliably, it can hin-
der the receiver’s understanding and decision-making. This
issue is particularly critical in applications involving visu-
ally impaired individuals, where misunderstandings or delays
could compromise personal safety. Therefore, addressing the
challenge of completing missing frames in received natural
language descriptions is crucial for ensuring reliable semantic
communication.

B. VISUAL DATA GENERATION
Visual data generation is a pivotal component in enabling
smart agents to interpret semantics and contextual nuances in
received text. This process is achieved through sophisticated
neural network models that transform textual descriptions
into corresponding visual representations, such as images.
Achieving a harmonious and logical alignment between the
generated images and their textual counterparts is a challeng-
ing endeavor due to the inherent ambiguity in correlating
images with natural language expressions and the necessity
to maintain a high level of image realism.
Prominent developments in this field include OpenAI’s
DALL-E model [35] and Google’s Imagen model [36], both
designed for text-to-image generation. A notable limitation
in these models is their computational efficiency, primarily
because they function in pixel space. To manage computa-
tional constraints, these models initially produce images of
64×64 resolution, which are subsequently upscaled to 256×
256 and finally to 1024 × 1024, utilizing super-resolution
techniques. A significant advancement is presented in [37],
which introduced the Stable Diffusion model. The core idea
is to start from the pure noise image and gradually recover to
the target image through a series of reverse diffusion steps.
Its operation in latent space offers enhanced computational
FIGURE 3. Visual data generation.
efficiency, making it a superior choice for integration into the
ULSC system.
The image generation process of the Stable Diffusion
model is illustrated in Fig.3. First, the user inputs a text
prompt, such as ‘‘a beach with palm trees and clear blue
water’’. This text prompt is processed through a pre-trained
CLIP text encoder, generating the corresponding text embed-
dings. The CLIP text encoder converts the text into a
multi-dimensional vector that effectively captures the seman-
tic information of the text. Meanwhile, an initial noise seed is
sampled from a standard Gaussian distribution. These noise
seeds serve as the initial input for the latent representa-
tion. The noise seeds are represented as a multi-dimensional
matrix of a specific size, forming the starting point of the
image generation process. Next, the noise seeds and text
embeddings are fed into the noise predictor, which uses a
U-Net model [38]. The U-Net model is a convolutional neural
network capable of denoising while preserving crucial details
in the image. At each timestep, the U-Net model predicts
and removes noise based on the current latent representa-
tion and text embeddings, gradually generating a new latent
representation. This process involves multiple iterative steps,
with each step progressively removing some noise, trans-
forming the latent representation from pure noise to a form
closer to the target image. Throughout the denoising process,
the text embeddings provide guidance for image generation,
ensuring that the generated image aligns with the input text
prompt. After completing all the denoising steps, the final
latent representation is decoded through a VAE decoder,
producing the final high-quality image. The VAE decoder
converts the denoised latent representation into a complete
image, significantly enhancing the detail and quality of the
image, which matches the initial text prompt. Through this
process, the Stable Diffusion model can start from random
noise, incorporate the input text prompt, and gradually gen-
erate a high-quality image that corresponds to the description.

VII. DISCUSSION
By introducing LLMs, our proposed ULSC system makes
significant progress in guaranteeing semantic transmission
and enhancing visual privacy. Although the current research
results are promising, there are still some key areas that
deserve further exploration and optimization to further
improve the performance and utility of the system.
A. SEMANTIC IMPORTANCE EVALUATION
In our current approach to semantic importance evaluation,
we have primarily focused on individual word assess-
ments. While this method has proven effective for many

applications, it falls short in capturing the contextual sig-
nificance of phrases or word combinations. For example,
in the sentence ‘‘A beach with palm trees and clear blue
water’’, the individual words ‘‘palm’’ and ‘‘trees’’ may
be undervalued due to their common co-occurrence. How-
ever, when considered as the phrase ‘‘palm trees’’, they
form a critical component of the scene’s description. This
discrepancy reveals a key limitation in our method: the poten-
tial omission of important semantic relationships between
words.
To overcome this limitation, we plan to explore seman-
tic importance evaluation method based on image semantic
segmentation [41]. For example, we consider an image that
the text describes as ‘‘a beach with palm trees and clear
blue water’’. Palm trees and other important elements in the
images can be identified by semantic segmentation. If the
segmentation results show that ‘‘palm trees’’ occupies a
prominent position in the vision, then the system will improve
the semantic importance of the phrase ‘‘palm trees’’ in the
overall text description. This method not only captures key-
words in the text, but also understands how these keywords
combine with other visual elements to form a complete
description of the scene. By integrating text and image, the
semantic importance of each part of text can be determined
more accurately, so as to improve the performance and effect
of semantic communication system.
B. SEMANTIC ERROR CORRECTION WITHOUT PRIOR
KNOWLEDGE OF ERROR LOCATIONS
In Section V, we introduced a BERT-based semantic error
correction method that relies on the receiver knowing exactly
where the data is lost. However, this assumption often fails
in practical applications. To solve this problem, a new error
correction mechanism can be introduced that does not require
prior knowledge of the error location.
Sequence number [42] and checksum [43] are two widely
used techniques that can help identify missing or corrupted
data frames during data transmission. Sequence number
ensures sequential data by assigning a unique identifier to
each packet. If the receiver detects a discontinuity in the
sequence number, it can determine that the data frame has
been lost. Checksum is hashed over the packet to generate a
short checksum, which is then appended to the data frame.
The receiver verifies the integrity of the data by recalculating
the checksum of the received data frame and comparing it
with the transmitted checksum.
The new mechanism first uses sequence number to detect
missing data frames. Once a data frame loss is detected, the
system will enter error correction mode. In error correction
mode, checksum is used to determine if the packet is corrupt.
If the data frame is corrupted, the system will attempt to
recover the lost data using a BERT-based semantic error
correction method. By introducing sequence number and
checksum, the system can perform efficient semantic error
correction without knowing the location of the error. This
improvement is expected to improve the practicability and
robustness of the designed system and make it more suitable
for practical communication environment.
C. MODEL CUSTOMIZATION AND DEVELOPMENT
In this paper, we utilize pre-trained models such as BERT
and ChatGPT due to their established effectiveness in natu-
ral language processing tasks. These models provide robust
semantic understanding, which enhances the accuracy of our
analysis and allows for efficient development [44]. Leverag-
ing these mature technologies enables us to focus on the core
innovations of our semantic communication framework.
We recognize the importance of customizing these
pre-trained models to better meet the specific needs of
semantic communication tasks. Our future work will involve
modifying model architectures and training processes to opti-
mize the conversion of visual data into natural language
descriptions. Additionally, we will design task-specific loss
functions and evaluation metrics to further refine model per-
formance. We also plan to fine-tune the models using relevant
datasets and explore transfer learning strategies to enhance
their adaptability to particular communication environments.
These efforts aim to strengthen the overall effectiveness of
our proposed system in addressing the challenges inherent in
semantic communication.
VIII. CONCLUSION
In this study, we proposed an innovative approach for
understanding-level semantic communications by integrat-
ing semantic importance-aware communication (SIAC) with
semantic correction using advanced LLMs such as BERT
and ChatGPT. Our method aligns image communication
within the human-comprehensible semantic space of nat-
ural language, enabling effective semantic correction and
significance analysis through LLMs. We developed a tech-
nique to quantify the semantic value of data in each frame,
supporting a semantically informed SIAC strategy. Addi-
tionally, the research explores a semantic-aware adaptive
modulation and coding scheme, complemented by a proposed
low-complexity greedy search method to ensure semantically
robust communications within set latency limits. The utiliza-
tion of LLMs for filling in missing frames is also introduced.
Experimental results validate the superiority of our approach
in enhancing agent-human communications or generative
task-oriented communications, particularly in terms of mini-
mizing semantic loss and bolstering privacy protection.
